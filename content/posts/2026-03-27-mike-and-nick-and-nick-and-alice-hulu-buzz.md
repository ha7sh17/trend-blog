## The basic plot (without spoiling every twist)

At the center is **Nick** (Vince Vaughn), a mob-connected hitman. Then another Nick arrives—from six months in the future. Future Nick returns to stop a chain of events involving betrayal, revenge, and a deadly setup targeting **Mike** (James Marsden), who is also romantically involved with **Alice** (Eiza González), Nick’s wife.

That creates the film’s core engine: two versions of the same man—one impulsive and violent, one regretful and desperate to undo the damage—colliding inside a criminal world run by a boss named **Sosa** (Keith David).

So yes, the movie is exactly what the title promises: Mike, one Nick, another Nick, and Alice locked in escalating chaos.

## Source angle #1: Mashable says it’s a bold, funny genre mash-up

Mashable’s take is strongly positive and positions the movie as a smartly offbeat entertainment play.

Key points from that review:

- Writer-director **BenDavid Grabinski** takes a “bold swing” blending gangster and sci-fi comedy elements.
- The cast is framed as a major strength, especially Vaughn’s two-version performance and supporting turns from Marsden and González.

Mashable’s argument is basically: this movie works because it commits to the bit, stays funny, and gives its cast a sandbox to play in.

For trend watchers, that kind of “must-see crowd pleaser” framing drives immediate sampling behavior—people start watching to see if they agree.

## Source angle #2: AV Club says it’s over-quirked and overworked

AV Club lands in nearly the opposite place, describing the movie as frenetic, overly self-conscious, and tonally messy.

Highlights from that review:

- The dialogue is criticized as aggressively “movie-ish,” with banter that feels more written than lived-in.
- Genre mixing is viewed as more marketing promise than narrative payoff (sci-fi, buddy comedy, action, gangster, romance all competing for space).
- While Vaughn’s performance gets meaningful credit, the review suggests the whole package doesn’t cohere.

This “good idea, uneven execution” angle matters because it creates argument-friendly friction online. Mixed consensus can generate more search demand than universal praise, since people want to test the debate themselves.

## Source angle #3: Boston Globe praises craft and old-school Vaughn energy

The Boston Globe review is notably enthusiastic and emphasizes both technical staging and emotional texture.

Its main themes:

- Vaughn’s dual role is treated as the film’s anchor, showing a believable shift between present-day volatility and future remorse.

That creates a third lane in public perception: not just “fun chaos” or “messy chaos,” but “chaos with craft.”

When you stack these three critical frames together, the trend becomes easy to understand: the movie is polarizing enough to be interesting, but mainstream enough to be accessible in one click on Hulu.
