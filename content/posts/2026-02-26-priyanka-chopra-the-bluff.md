## What The Bluff Is About

*The Bluff* is a period action drama set in the 19th-century Caribbean. Priyanka Chopra plays Ercell “Bloody Mary” Bodden, a former pirate trying to live a quieter family life. That calm breaks when violent men from her past arrive, looking for control, revenge, and treasure.

The movie leans into survival-action structure: island setting, escalating attacks, improvised combat, and a central lead forced back into an old identity. Karl Urban plays Captain Connor, a menacing figure tied to Ercell’s past, while the story builds around whether she can protect her husband, children, and community.

The film is directed by Frank E. Flowers and produced with involvement from the Russo brothers’ production banner—something frequently highlighted in coverage as part of its action credentials.

## What Critics Are Saying (And Why Reactions Are Split)

One reason this trend has legs is the gap between what critics liked and what they didn’t.

### 1) Strong response to Chopra’s physical performance

Across multiple reviews, Chopra’s commitment to the role’s physicality is a recurring positive. The Guardian’s review emphasizes that she throws herself into the film’s gritty choreography and “pulpiness,” even when the script gives her limited dramatic material. In other words, critics may disagree on the overall film quality, but many agree she delivers as an action lead.

The New York Times review also frames Chopra as the standout element, highlighting her “admirable feistiness” and credibility in the movie’s most violent confrontations.

### 2) Action praised, writing questioned

Coverage summarized by ComingSoon (and syndicated on Yahoo) captures the consensus tension: *The Bluff* is often described as efficient and visceral, but narratively thin. Several quoted critics call out the film’s bare-bones script while still praising its momentum and fight design.

That split usually creates social traction. Viewers who enjoy straightforward, high-energy genre movies can still have a good time, while critics who prioritize character depth may come away colder.

### 3) Rotten Tomatoes score reinforces “mixed” positioning

As release-day coverage circulated, *The Bluff* was reported at around **64% on the Tomatometer** (with limited review volume at that stage). That score is not a disaster, but it does place the film in the “divided reception” zone.

For streaming releases, that can still be commercially useful: mixed reviews often increase curiosity because audiences want to decide for themselves.
