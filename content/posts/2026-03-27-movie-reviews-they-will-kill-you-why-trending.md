If you noticed **“movie reviews”** climbing Google Trends in the U.S. today, there’s a clear trigger: critics all dropped reactions to **_They Will Kill You_**, and audiences started searching fast.

The film, directed by **Kirill Sokolov** and starring **Zazie Beetz** and **Myha’la**, landed in that sweet spot of internet attention where one movie is polarizing enough to generate curiosity, but fun enough to spark “okay, now I need to see this” momentum.

In short, this is one of those trend spikes where the keyword looks broad (“movie reviews”), but the real conversation is highly specific.

Here’s what happened, what major outlets are saying, and why this review cycle turned into a viral search moment.

## Why “movie reviews” is trending right now

Today’s trend is being driven by three things happening at once:

1. **Multiple high-profile outlets reviewed the same title on the same day** (The New York Times, Rolling Stone, The Hollywood Reporter).
2. **The movie has a highly clickable premise**: rich Manhattan elites + satanic ritual + revenge action + nonstop gore.
3. **Review disagreement is strong enough to fuel debate** — some critics praise the wild style and Beetz’s performance, while others say the storytelling is thin and derivative.

That combination almost always creates search behavior: people want spoiler-light context before deciding whether to buy a ticket or stream later.

## The movie at the center of the trend: _They Will Kill You_

At a basic plot level, _They Will Kill You_ follows **Asia** (Zazie Beetz), a woman who enters the ultra-exclusive **Virgil** apartment building in Manhattan under false pretenses to rescue her younger sister **Maria** (Myha’la). Inside, the residents are tied to violent occult rituals, and Asia has to fight floor by floor to survive.

Runtime is a tight **94 minutes**, and almost every major review notes the same tonal blend:

- action-comedy-horror
- class satire (“eat-the-rich” framing)
- grindhouse-inspired blood-and-chaos energy

So yes — this is intentionally not subtle cinema. It’s built for momentum, spectacle, and visceral set pieces.

## What critics agree on

Even with different final verdicts, the reviews share some surprisingly consistent takeaways.

### 1) Zazie Beetz is the main reason to watch

Rolling Stone and THR are especially direct on this point: Beetz carries the movie physically and emotionally. The action choreography is described as energetic, committed, and often the best part of the experience.

Rolling Stone frames the film almost like a proof-of-concept for Beetz as an above-the-title action lead. THR similarly highlights her athletic screen presence and charisma, even when the script itself gets chaotic.

### 2) The gore is not just present — it’s the point

The New York Times calls it a splatter-fest. THR talks about “great big arterial sprays.” Rolling Stone leans into the grindhouse flavor and over-the-top brutality.

If you’re gore-averse, this is likely not your movie.
If you enjoy stylized mayhem and practical-looking carnage, critics suggest you’ll get what you came for.

### 3) The setting has strong concept value

The Virgil building is repeatedly described as an effective container for tension: locked spaces, escalating danger, and level-by-level progression. Several critics compare this structure to video game progression or vertical action films like _The Raid_ / _Dredd_-style movement through hostile territory.

That architectural structure gives the film immediate pacing clarity: keep moving up, keep surviving.

## Where critics split hard

This is where trend energy really comes from.

### A) Style vs. originality

Rolling Stone argues the movie’s influences are obvious — Tarantino, exploitation cinema, neo-grindhouse — and suggests it sometimes feels more like remix than invention.

THR is a bit more forgiving, saying Sokolov’s influences are still enjoyable when paired with kinetic staging and sheer commitment.

The Times appears the most skeptical about writing depth, suggesting the visual and prosthetic creativity outpaces narrative substance.

### B) Social commentary depth

All three pieces acknowledge class politics in the premise: wealthy residents exploiting vulnerable workers. But critics disagree on whether the film meaningfully explores this.

THR’s blunt summary is that the class commentary is there mostly to make extreme violence feel justified, not to build nuanced critique.

For many viewers, that may be fine. But for critics expecting sharper satire, it lands as surface-level.

### C) Character development

A recurring complaint in reviews is that supporting characters are underdeveloped and some emotional arcs are rushed. The relationship between Asia and Maria has clear dramatic potential, but some critics feel the movie prioritizes spectacle over character payoff.

Again, whether this is a flaw or a feature depends on what you want from an action-horror film.

## The quote moments that are driving clicks

Several review lines are especially quoteable and likely fueling social reposts:

- NYT: describes a “splatter-fest” where creative effects may exceed writing depth.
- Rolling Stone: calls it a revenge movie with a “massive Tarantino fetish.”
- THR: “Not a lot of brains, but plenty of splattered guts.”

These aren’t neutral summaries — they’re strong takes. Strong takes travel fast, and fast-sharing quotes often turn into trend spikes for broad terms like “movie reviews.”

## Why this trend matters beyond one film

At first glance, “movie reviews” looks generic. But this spike reveals a broader 2026 pattern in entertainment search behavior:

1. **People don’t wait for aggregated scores anymore.** They read 2–3 trusted takes and decide quickly.
2. **Polarizing reviews are more viral than unanimous praise.** Disagreement creates curiosity.
3. **Action-horror with a clear visual identity spreads faster on social media** than prestige dramas with subtle hooks.

That means movies like _They Will Kill You_ can win attention even if critics are mixed — because attention now follows conversation velocity, not just consensus.

## Should you watch _They Will Kill You_?

Based on the review landscape, here’s the practical recommendation:

### Watch it if you like:

- high-energy, close-quarters action
- stylized gore and grindhouse aesthetics
- revenge-thriller momentum
- watching Zazie Beetz in full action-star mode

### Skip (or wait) if you need:

- layered character development
- subtle satire with strong thematic follow-through
- highly original plotting

A fair expectation-setter is this: think of it as a **style-forward midnight movie** with an excellent lead performance, not a deeply literary horror narrative.

“Movie reviews” is trending because _They Will Kill You_ triggered the perfect digital storm: a buzzy genre setup, a magnetic lead performance, and sharply divided critical framing.

## FAQ

### Why is “movie reviews” trending on Google Trends today?

### What is _They Will Kill You_ about?
It follows Asia (Zazie Beetz), who infiltrates a luxury Manhattan building to rescue her sister and ends up fighting through satanic elite residents in a violent survival scenario.

### Is _They Will Kill You_ getting good reviews?
Reviews are mixed-to-positive on entertainment value. Most critics praise Beetz and the action choreography, while several criticize thin plotting and limited thematic depth.

### Is this a serious horror movie or more of a fun gore movie?

### Is the class satire in the movie strong?
Critics acknowledge the “eat-the-rich” angle, but some argue it remains surface-level and mostly serves as a setup for violent set pieces.

### Should I watch it in theaters?
